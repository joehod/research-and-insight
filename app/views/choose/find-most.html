Choosing a visa is easier when there is a close match between activity and type of visa as users feel reassured what they want to do is permitted. Well named visas help with this.

Evidence:

User research

UKVI services on GOV.UK

Researchers: Clare Ridd and Joe Coker
Participants: 12 interviews with visit visa holders
Date: October 2017

Some users had read all the visas and chosen theirs by a process of elimination:

  • "It was not so quickly to know which visa I needed… finally I got standard visa”]; “I looked at all the other visas and I did not fall into any of the categories … in the end I followed by instincts and went for the tourist visa” [R6 - P12]

This lead to an inefficient, time-consuming process and reduced confidence in having made the right choice. There was a sense of users looking for the application that matched their circumstance and knowing it when they saw it.

  • "There were many options but this was the only option that was relevant to me” [R6 - P10]
  • “Once I saw the relevant application form it was more straightforward” [R6 - P5].

Choosing a visa is easier when there is a close match between activity and type of visa. Someone coming to do a training course with their company got confused as to whether this was came under a business visit or a student visit:

  • “I was a little bit confused between a business visa and the visa given for student doing a short-term course”.

Another was not clear whether their business trip constituted work and they needed to go to the full effort of a work visa:

  • “there is another visa I think takes more time and effort to get…I needed something simpler because I was not going to work”

Conversely, it is easier when close match between activity and type of visa. Well named visas help with this:

  • “The names of the choices, when you try they look a bit more convenient”

Users had clear ideas about whether they were or were not a tourist and this influenced their decisions:

  • “I’m not a tourist, I’m going to visit my girlfriend”
  • “here was lots of options Like standard visa or this visiting as an academic stuff. So, I was confused. Which one am I applying for, because I’m going for an excursion” [R6 - P9]

Operational and Policy context:

Up until 2008 there were a larger number of visit visas avaialable. This included:

  • Family Visitor visa
  • General Visitor visa
  • Child Visitor visa
  • Business Visitor visa, including visas for academics, doctors and dentists
  • Sports Visitor visa
  • Entertainer Visitor visa
  • Prospective Entrepreneur visa
  • Private Medical Treatment Visitor visa
  • Approved Destination Status (ADS) visa

These were all brought under the Standard Visitor Visa that is avaialable today. This was to simplify the guidance to a single source rather than providing individual guidance for each of indidivudal visit visas avaialble

Impact:

On individual users

User research

UKVI services on GOV.UK

Researchers: Clare Ridd and Joe Coker
Participants: 12 interviews with visit visa holders
Date: October 2017

Confusion:

  • "So, I was confused. Which one am I applying for, because I’m going for an excursion” [R6-P9]

Anxiety:

  • "I was afraid that if I selected the wrong type, it could cause more delay in our visa application" [R6-P3]
  • "There’s a side of me that kept on saying, What if I didn’t get the right visa?"" [R6-10]

On performance measures

It may be the case that a number of calls to contact centres are the result of potential visit visa applicants wanting reassurance that they have selected the correct visa for what they want to do.

Recommendations:

1) Commission research to explore potential benefit of alternative naming practices for visit visas

2) Collect the reason for calls to teh contact centre to identify the prevalence of users calling because they cannot confidently identify the correct visa for what they want to do
